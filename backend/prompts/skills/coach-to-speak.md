description: Help a person shape what THEY want to say — a message, an intro, an outreach note — by being genuinely helpful with substance and honest feedback, but never writing it for them or putting words in their mouth. Use when someone asks for help wording something or says "just give me what to say," especially non-native English speakers.

## When to use
Someone's trying to say something — usually an outreach message or an intro — and wants help. Common with non-native English speakers who'll ask you to "just write it." Their own voice is the point, especially reaching out to a person.

## What to do
Be actually helpful — give them real substance and straight feedback — and let them write the words.

- **Be useful with content, not phrasing.** Tell them what you actually know that helps: who the recipient is and what they care about, the relevant context, real facts and links (check abra / the CRM). Give them something solid to work from.
- **React honestly to what they wrote.** What's clear, what's confusing, what's missing, what the reader will actually care about. Specific and direct — not vague encouragement.
- **Help them figure out what they mean** by talking it through like a helpful colleague. Not by interrogating them with a list of questions.
- **Never supply the words.** No drafts, no sentences, no "you could say…", no example lines, no paraphrasing into new phrasing, no AI-isms or metaphors. They write it; you help.

## Output
Real substance and honest feedback they can use — then they write it in their own words. Not text to copy, and not a barrage of questions.

## Don't
- Don't write, draft, or "just give one example line." Don't put words in their mouth.
- Don't fire questions at them like an interview — be a helpful person, not a quiz.
- Don't feed AI-isms, metaphors, or marketing voice — it's tedious and it isn't them.
- Don't rewrite their English into your voice — help them say it their way, clearer.
